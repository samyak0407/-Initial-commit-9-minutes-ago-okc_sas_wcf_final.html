San Antonio takes the West

The Spurs won three of the last four games — including a road close-out in Oklahoma City — to advance to the NBA Finals against New York. Victor Wembanyama led the series in every meaningful efficiency category. Here's the seven-game story, in numbers.

It took seven games to settle the West, and on Saturday night in Oklahoma City, San Antonio finished what they started in Game 1. The Spurs won the deciding game 111–103, sealing a 4–3 series victory over the Thunder and a trip to the NBA Finals.

The series produced exactly what the data suggested it would: a tight, swingy contest where no game was particularly close (none decided by fewer than seven), where each team imposed its preferred style alternately, and where in the end the team with the better superstar and the deeper supporting cast — San Antonio — found one more answer than the team with the regular-season trophy.

Three numbers tell the story before any analysis begins. San Antonio outscored Oklahoma City by 26 points across the seven games (789–763). Victor Wembanyama finished the series with a +62 plus-minus; Shai Gilgeous-Alexander finished at −35. And in six of the seven games, the team that posted the higher true-shooting percentage won — Game 2, where OKC won despite slightly worse efficiency than San Antonio, was the lone exception.

Look at the rhythm of the series: SAS, OKC, OKC, SAS, OKC, SAS, SAS. The Spurs won the opener on the road. The Thunder responded with two consecutive wins, including a road win of their own in Game 3. Then San Antonio took control with a blowout, OKC pulled back to a 3–2 lead, and the Spurs closed it out with their biggest win of the series in Game 6 before clinching with a more measured Game 7. Of the four SAS wins, none were close. Of the three OKC wins, all were comfortable. There were zero clutch finishes in this series.

02 · The Game 7

How San Antonio won the West

The deciding game was the kind of Game 7 that doesn't fit the cliché — it was neither a defensive grinder nor a wire-to-wire shootout. San Antonio led 32–25 after the first quarter, traded the middle two quarters, and then closed Oklahoma City out 31–26 in the fourth.

Figure 02
Game 7 — quarter-by-quarter
San Antonio won quarters 1 and 4. The middle two were essentially split.
OKC SAS

The shooting numbers tell the story cleanly. San Antonio shot 17-of-40 from three (42.5%) — their best long-range game of the series. Julian Champagnie was the star of the line, hitting 6-of-10 from three for 20 points and a +16 plus/minus, the highest mark on either team. Wembanyama added 22 points on efficient shooting, including 3-of-5 from three. Stephon Castle, Dylan Harper, and Devin Vassell each scored 11 to 16 points.

Shai Gilgeous-Alexander had a brilliant individual game — 35 points on 12-of-21 (57.1%) with 9 assists — but the math didn't help his team. He was −7 on the floor, the worst plus/minus of any OKC starter. The supporting cast couldn't keep up: Alex Caruso, who had been efficient all series, went a stunning 3-of-14 from the field (21.4%) and finished −5. The OKC bench scored 37 points (still more than San Antonio's 27), but they couldn't convert it into a win.

The structural problem for Oklahoma City was at the rim: they shot just 5-of-12 (41.7%) from inside, the worst rim conversion they posted in the entire series. Without easy buckets at the basket — typically a strength — they were forced into deep jumpers that didn't fall at the rate they needed.

Game 7 in one sentence

SAS shot 42.5% from three and got contributions from five players; OKC got 35 from their star and not enough from anyone else.

03 · The series, by category

Two teams, two different games

Across seven games, the structural picture stayed consistent. San Antonio won the rim, the boards, and the free-throw line. Oklahoma City won the bench scoring battle and the three-point percentage edge. Neither team dominated on raw shooting percentage — and yet the team that won the efficiency battle on a given night always won the game.

Figure 03
Per-game category averages
Where each team's structural edge lies, averaged over all seven games.
OKC SAS

Several gaps stand out. San Antonio averaged 60.8% at the rim to OKC's 55.3% — a 5.5 point gap that, multiplied across roughly 28 rim attempts per game, translates to about three points of margin per game. The Spurs also averaged 16 fast-break points to the Thunder's 11, and 26 free-throw attempts to 24. Oklahoma City's structural advantage was bench scoring: 47.4 points to 28.6, a +18.8 nightly edge that should have been decisive but wasn't enough to overcome the rim and rebound gaps.

One genuinely surprising finding: both teams shot the three-point shot at exactly the same percentage across the series — 34.1%. They diverged only in volume (OKC took 255 threes to SAS's 279) and in who took them — which is the part that mattered.

Figure 04
What predicts margin of victory
Correlation of each stat with point margin across all 14 team-games.

The top predictors of winning, in order, were offensive rating (correlation +0.625), field-goal percentage (+0.571), true shooting percentage (+0.565), and assists (+0.554). Three-point percentage came in seventh (+0.518). Fast-break points correlated essentially zero (+0.066) — meaningful volume of fast-break offense did not translate to winning. Steals correlated at +0.015, statistical noise. The series was decided by half-court efficiency and ball movement, not by transition or perimeter pressure.

04 · Shot zones

Different shot diets

The zone breakdown captures the offensive identity of each team. Both shoot more than half of their classified attempts from three. But where they go for their two-point looks differs starkly. Note: the box-score categories shown here (rim, midrange, three) cover about 79% of OKC's attempts and 86% of SAS's — non-rim paint two-pointers fall outside these three buckets.

Figure 05
Field goal percentage by zone
San Antonio dominates at the rim. Three-point conversion is identical.
OKC SAS

San Antonio finished the series shooting 60.8% at the rim. Oklahoma City shot 55.3%. The Spurs' rim advantage (+5.5 percentage points) compounded across the series — they took 199 rim attempts to OKC's 152, a 47-shot volume edge as well. In the playoffs, that's the difference between Wembanyama, Castle, and Harper getting downhill consistently versus the Thunder being forced to settle for jumpers, which happened repeatedly in their losses.

Midrange shooting was the inverse pattern: OKC shot 43.5% from there to San Antonio's 36.5%, but with 85 OKC attempts to 52 SAS attempts. The Thunder kept the midrange game alive as a counter; the Spurs essentially abandoned it. San Antonio took 52 midrange attempts in 7 games to OKC's 85 — a deliberately extreme shot diet that funneled more touches to the rim and the three-point line.

05 · Player rankings

The real impact leaders

Series plus-minus tells a clear hierarchy. Wembanyama anchored the entire San Antonio operation. Three more Spurs (Harper, Champagnie, Vassell) finished with +34 or better in plus-minus. The Thunder had only one player (Alex Caruso) crack +30. That depth-of-impact difference shows up everywhere in the team-level numbers.

Wembanyama is in a tier of his own — 27.3 PPG at 62.0% TS, a combination that no other star in the series approached. SGA scored almost as much (25.9 PPG) but on dramatically lower efficiency (54.6% TS), and was a −35 in the series — the worst plus-minus among the high-volume scorers, a stunning number for the league MVP from the season prior. 06 · Win recipes

Two paths, two outcomes

The single most useful exercise across the seven games is comparing each team's stats in their wins versus their losses. The patterns are not subtle.

Figure 07
OKC's win recipe
When OKC won (3 games) vs lost (4 games). Their wins required everything to click on offense.
In 3 wins In 4 losses

OKC's win recipe was almost entirely offensive: when they won, their true shooting jumped from 49% to 62%, their three-point percentage from 29% to 41%, their assists from 22.5 to 29.7, and — most strikingly — their rim conversion from 48% to 66%. Their bench scoring went from 40 to 58. Defensive rebounding barely changed. In other words, OKC won when their offense was on fire and lost when it was even slightly off.

Figure 08
SAS's win recipe
When SAS won (4 games) vs lost (3 games). Their wins were built on defense, not offense.
In 4 wins In 3 losses

San Antonio's win recipe is dramatically different — and reveals why they're the team going to the Finals. Defensive rebounds jumped from 27.7 to 37.5 (+9.8) in wins. Total rebounds from 41.0 to 51.2 (+10.2). Turnovers dropped from 17.3 to 13.0. But shooting percentages actually went down in wins — TS% 55.7 vs 57.5, rim 58.3% vs 65.5%. The Spurs didn't win by shooting better; they won by playing defense and controlling possessions. That's a far more replicable formula, and it's the formula that will travel to New York for the Finals.

The structural verdict

OKC needed everything to click to win. SAS only needed to play defense. One of those formulas is more replicable than the other — and it's the one going to the Finals.

07 · The verdict

Why San Antonio actually won

Reduce the series to a single sentence and it reads like this: San Antonio had the best player, more efficient role players, a clearer identity, and a more replicable formula for winning. Oklahoma City had the home-court advantage and the regular-season pedigree, and neither was enough.

Three structural truths emerged across the seven games.

One: Wembanyama is now the best player in the West. 27.3 PPG, 10.9 RPG, 62.0% TS, 40.0% from three on 40 attempts, and a +62 plus-minus over seven games. The combination of volume, efficiency, and impact is unmatched anywhere in the data. He single-handedly anchored both the Spurs' offense and their rim protection. Game 4 alone (33 points, +29) was probably the best individual performance in the series.

Two: OKC's roster construction failed when it mattered. SGA scored the second-most in the series but on inefficient volume and with a −35 plus-minus. Caruso was brilliant but cooled at the worst time. Hartenstein (−33) and Dort (−63) were liabilities all series — both finished bottom-five in plus-minus despite playing rotation minutes. When the Thunder's bench is winning the points battle by 19 per game and they still lose the series, the issue is the starters.

Three: The asymmetric ceilings argument held. The pre-Game-7 analysis flagged that Wembanyama's ceiling was higher than SGA's. Game 7 confirmed it not because Wemby was dominant (he was good, not great, with 22 points) but because the supporting cast he led was structurally deeper. Champagnie's 20-point three-point clinic was the kind of game OKC didn't have in their back pocket.

The Thunder won 64 games. The Spurs won the series. Regular-season chalk meant less than positional matchups, and the positional matchups favored San Antonio from the moment the series started. The series in retrospect

San Antonio now faces a New York team that swept Cleveland 4-0 in the Eastern Conference Finals and has been waiting since May 25 for its opponent. The Spurs will be on short rest. They'll be the lower seed. They'll be playing a Knicks team with elite half-court defense.

The Finals start Wednesday in New York. That's a different story for a different piece. For now, the West has its champion: Victor Wembanyama and the San Antonio Spurs.
